## CRM (Customer Relationship Management)

CRM = strategic process organizations use to manage, analyze, + improve customer interactions. Data-driven → dedicated systems store + analyze customer data, support communication, coordinate sales, marketing, service.

CRM systems compile data from: website, phone (softphone), email, live chat, marketing materials, social media. Allow businesses to learn target audiences + cater to needs → retain customers + drive sales growth. Applies to past, present, | potential customers. Covers: direct contact (sales, service), forecasting, analysis of consumer patterns + behavior.

Global CRM market: $101.41B (2024) → $262.74B (2032), CAGR 12.6%.

## History

CRM concept originated early 1970s — customer satisfaction evaluated via annual surveys | front-line asking. Businesses relied on standalone mainframe systems to automate sales; technology limited to categorizing customers in spreadsheets + lists.

Notable precursor: Farley File — developed by James Farley (FDR's campaign manager). Comprehensive records of political + personal facts about people FDR met | was to meet.

1982: Kate + Robert D. Kestenbaum introduced database marketing — statistical methods to analyze + gather customer data.
1987: Pat Sullivan + Mike Muhney released ACT! — customer evaluation system based on digital Rolodex; first contact management service.

1993: Tom Siebel (Siebel Systems) designed first CRM product: Siebel Customer Relationship Management. ERP vendors (Oracle, Zoho Corporation, SAP, Peoplesoft, Navision) extended capabilities with embedded CRM modules — sales force automation + extended customer service (inquiry, activity management).

1997: CRM popularized via Siebel, Gartner, IBM. 1997–2000: leading products enriched with shipping + marketing capabilities.
1999: Siebel launched first mobile CRM app — Siebel Sales Handheld.
Cloud-hosted customer base adopted by PeopleSoft (acquired by Oracle), Oracle, SAP, Salesforce.com.

2004: SugarCRM developed first open-source CRM. CRM migrated rapidly to cloud → accessible to sole entrepreneurs + small teams → large price reduction.

~2009: developers built tools for social media; startups provided social CRM solutions (Base, Nutshell). Gartner organized first CRM Summit; defined features required for CRM classification.

2013–2014: popular CRM products linked to business intelligence + communication software. Trend: replace standardized solutions with industry-specific | customizable ones.
Nov 2016: Forrester identified nine most significant CRM suites from eight prominent vendors.

## Types

### Strategic

Strategic CRM: development of customer-centric business culture. Customer-centric design + implementation of CRM strategy → ↑ CLV.

### Operational

Primary goal: integration + automation of sales, marketing, customer support. Systems provide dashboard: overall view of three functions on single customer view. Dashboard includes client info, past sales, previous marketing efforts, all relationship data.

Operational CRM = three components:

- Sales force automation: covers all sales cycle stages — entering contact info → converting prospective client to actual client. Implements sales promotion analysis, automates account history tracking for repeat | future sales, coordinates sales/marketing/call centers/retail. Prevents duplicate efforts; automatically tracks all contacts + follow-ups.
- Marketing automation: eases marketing process → more effective + efficient. Automates repeated tasks (e.g., scheduled marketing emails, social media posts). Goal: convert sales lead → full customer. CRM systems also engage customers via social media.
- Service automation: focuses on direct customer service technology. Supports customers across channels: phone, email, knowledge bases, ticketing portals, FAQs.

### Analytical

Analytical CRM: analyzes customer data from multiple sources → presents for informed management decisions. Techniques: data mining, correlation, pattern recognition. Analytics identify small problems → targeted marketing to consumer segments.

Example: analysis of buying behavior reveals ↓ purchases → company markets differently to that subset.

### Collaborative

Third primary aim: incorporate external stakeholders (suppliers, vendors, distributors) + share customer info across groups, departments, organizations. Example: feedback from technical support calls → informs future marketing direction.

### Customer Data Platform

CDP (customer data platform) = computer system used by marketing departments — assembles data about individuals from various sources into one database for interaction by other software systems. As of Feb 2017: ~20 companies selling such systems, revenue ~$300M.

## Components

Main CRM components:
- Build + manage customer relationships via marketing
- Observe relationships through distinct phases
- Manage relationships at each stage
- Recognize that relationship value distribution to firm is ¬ homogeneous

Firms benefit from tools to optimize organizational design, incentive schemes, customer structures, marketing reach. Recognizing distinct CRM phases → businesses see multiple relationships as connected transactions. Accounting for profitability of customer relationships → allocate different resources + attention by customer type. CRM systems may also automate repetitive tasks: data entry, follow-ups, customer segmentation.

Relational Intelligence = awareness of relationship variety a customer can have with a firm + ability to reinforce | change those connections. Important component of main CRM phases. Firms may capture demographic data (gender, age, income, education) + link to purchase data → profitability tiers — this is only industrial view. Lack of relational intelligence → firms see customers as up-sell | cross-sell resources ¬ individuals seeking personalized interactions.

CRM systems include:
- Data warehouse technology — aggregates transaction info, merges with CRM products, provides KPIs
- Opportunity management — manages unpredictable growth + demand, implements forecasting model integrating sales history + projections
- Campaign tracking systems — track + measure marketing campaigns across networks by customer clicks + sales
- SaaS delivery — web-based, subscription fee, ¬ local install
- Small business systems — integrate emails, documents, jobs, faxes, scheduling per account; legal/finance variants focus on event management + relationship tracking ¬ ROI
- eCommerce systems — focus on marketing automation: cart rescue, email re-engagement, personalization
- CCRM (customer-centric relationship management) — focuses on customer preferences ¬ leverage; adds value via individual, interactive relationships
- Non-profit + membership systems — track constituents, fundraising, sponsor demographics, membership levels + directories, volunteering, individual communication

CRM ¬ technology + strategy alone — integrated approach including employee knowledge + organizational culture.

## Effect on Customer Satisfaction

Customer satisfaction → ↑ loyalty + usage behavior, ↓ complaints + defection risk. CRM implementation likely affects customer satisfaction + knowledge.

Firms can customize offerings per customer. CRM accumulates interaction data → discovers hidden patterns → customizes offerings to individual tastes → ↑ perceived quality → ↑ customer satisfaction (indirect). CRM also enables timely, accurate processing of orders + requests + ongoing account management.

Example: Wyndham uses IT tools (via Piccoli + Applegate analysis) to deliver consistent service experience across properties. ↑ customization + ↓ consumption variability → ↑ perceived quality → ↑ satisfaction. CRM also manages relationships across: initiation, maintenance, termination stages.

### Customer Benefits

With CRM: customers served day-to-day; ↑ reliable info → ↓ need for self-service from companies → ↑ satisfaction level. Core CRM benefits connect to three equity types: relationship, value, brand → customer equity.

Eight recognized value drivers:
- ↑ ability to target profitable customers
- Integrated assistance across channels
- ↑ sales force efficiency + effectiveness
- Improved pricing
- Customized products + services
- ↑ customer service efficiency + effectiveness
- Individualized marketing messages (campaigns)
- Connect customers + all channels on single platform

### Examples

5% ↑ customer retention → 50% ↑ lifetime customer profits on average across industries; up to 90% in specific industries (e.g., insurance).

MBNA Europe: 75% annual profit growth since 1995. Invests heavily in screening potential cardholders → retains 97% of profitable customers. Card usage 52% above industry norm; avg expenditure 30% more per transaction; 10% of account holders request cross-sale info.

Amazon: implemented personal greetings, collaborative filtering → up to 80% customer repeat rate.

## Customer Profile

Customer profile = detailed description of a customer classification representing typical users of a product | service. Customer profiling: understand customers via demographics, behavior, lifestyle → customer-focused decisions ¬ personal opinion bias. Overall profiling: gather consumption habits → project into future → group for marketing + advertising.

Customer profiles = core data (name, address, company) + processed analytics data — a form of profiling.

Three basic profiling methods:
- Psychographic approach
- Consumer typology approach
- Consumer characteristics approach

These methods help design business around customer identity + support better customer-centered decisions.

## Improving CRM

Consultants: companies must establish strong CRM systems to ↑ relational intelligence. People have many different relationship types with different brands. Research study: relationships between consumers in China, Germany, Spain, US — 200+ brands, 11 industries (airlines, cars, media) → provides demographic, behavioral, value-based segmentation. Relationships can be positive | negative; customers may identify as friends, enemies, | mixed (love-hate). Range from distant to intimate.

### Data Analysis

Managers must understand reasons for relationship types → provide what customer seeks. Data collection methods: surveys, interviews. Companies store + receive large volumes of data via emails, chat sessions, phone calls — often underutilized. These signals indicate desired relationship type → companies should invest in relational intelligence. Tools: data mining, web searches, social media (networking sites, blogs, forums) → convert customer signals to actionable knowledge.

### Employee Training

Firms implement training: recognize + create strong customer-brand relationships. Some employees trained in social psychology + social sciences to bolster relationships. Customer service reps: trained to value relationships + understand existing customer profiles. Finance + legal departments: must also learn to manage + build customer relationships.

## In Practice

### Call Centers

Contact centre CRM: popular for small + mid-market businesses. Systems codify interactions via analytics + KPIs → inform marketing + customer service focus. Agents access caller history → personalized communication. Goal: ↑ avg revenue per user, ↓ churn rate, ↓ idle contact.

Gamification: game design elements in customer service (rewards, bonus points to reps as feedback). Motivates agents via desire for rewards, recognition, achievements, competition.

### Contact-Center Automation

CCA (contact-center automation) = integrated system coordinating contacts between organization + public → ↓ repetitive + tedious tasks. Pre-recorded audio messages help customers self-solve. Example: automated routing through command menus → specialist agent for customer's issue. Software tools integrate with agent desktop to handle questions + requests → saves employee time.

### Social Media

Social CRM: uses social media + technology to engage + learn from consumers. Companies use social networking sites to draw attention to products, services, brands → ↑ demand + customer relationships. Social media integration can be quicker + more cost-efficient than traditional CRM.

Some CRM systems integrate: Twitter, LinkedIn, Facebook — track + communicate with customers; customers share opinions + experiences → firms gain insight + track sentiment.

Enterprise feedback management platforms: combine internal survey data + social media trends → more accurate product supply decisions.

### Location-Based Services

CRM systems can include geographic marketing campaign technology. Takes in customer physical location data; integrates with GPS applications. Used for networking, contact management, ↑ location-based sales.

### Business-to-Business Transactions

CRM applies to B2B environments to streamline + improve customer management. B2B CRM must be personalized + delivered at individual levels. B2C vs B2B differences: contact database sizing, relationship length.

## Market Trends

### Social Networking

Gartner CRM Summit 2010: discussed capturing data from social networking traffic (Twitter, Facebook, other platforms); solutions provided to bring more clientele. Era of "social customer" = use of social media by customers.

### Mobile

Some CRM systems equipped with mobile capabilities → information accessible to remote sales staff.

### Cloud Computing and SaaS

Many CRM vendors offer subscription-based web tools (cloud computing) + SaaS. Salesforce.com: first to provide enterprise applications via web browser; maintained leadership. SaaS providers ↑ over years — CRM leading category for 2024.

Traditional providers moved to cloud via acquisitions:
- Oracle purchased RightNow (Oct 2011), Taleo + Eloqua (2012)
- SAP acquired SuccessFactors (Dec 2011)
- NetSuite acquired Verenia (2022)

### Sales and Sales Force Automation

Sales forces play important role in CRM — ↑ sales effectiveness + productivity drives CRM adoption. Top 2021 CRM trends: customer service automation (chatbots), hyper-personalization, unified CRM systems. CRM vendors support sales productivity with ad-effectiveness measurement tools (e.g., 3D video game ads).

Pharma companies: early SFA investors, some on third-/fourth-generation implementations. Deployments historically limited to SFA scope.

### Vendor Relationship Management

VRM (vendor relationship management) = tools + services allowing customers to manage individual vendor relationships. Grown from ProjectVRM (Harvard's Berkman Center for Internet & Society) + Identity Commons' Internet Identity Workshops + startups + established companies. VRM: cover story in CRM Magazine, May 2010.

### Customer Success

Customer Success ↑ as discipline. More companies establish Customer Success teams separate from Sales → manage existing customer relations. Drives demand for holistic customer health understanding — limitation for many existing vendors. ∴ new entrants enter market; existing vendors add capabilities.

### AI and Predictive Analytics

2017: artificial intelligence + predictive analytics identified as newest CRM trends.

## Criticism

Companies face large challenges implementing CRM systems. Consumer companies frequently manage customer relationships haphazardly + unprofitably. May ¬ effectively use customer connections due to misunderstandings of CRM analysis. Clients may be treated as exchange parties ¬ unique individuals due to gap between CRM data + analysis output. Studies show customers frequently frustrated by company inability to meet relationship expectations; companies ¬ always know how to translate CRM data → feasible action plan.

2003 Gartner report: $2B+ spent on unused software. CSO Insights: <40% of 1,275 companies had end-user adoption >90%. Many corporations use CRM only partially | fragmentarily. 2007 UK survey: 4/5 senior executives cited staff adoption as biggest challenge; 43% use <50% of existing system functionality.

Customer data collection (PII) must comply with privacy laws → extra legal expenditure.

CRM paradox ("dark side of CRM"): may entail favoritism + differential treatment — companies prioritize more profitable | relationship-oriented | loyal customers → other customers feel excluded → ↓ profits.

CRM technologies become ineffective without proper management + correct implementation. Data sets must be connected, distributed, organized → users access needed info quickly. Studies show ↑ customer dissatisfaction with contact center experiences due to lags + wait times. Customers demand multiple communication channels with seamless information transfer. ∴ cross-channel customer experience must be consistent + reliable.
